## Featured Playshops (full descriptions)

### Improvisation Across the Arts
Einstein said play is the highest form of research. He understood the interdisciplinarity of playing with ideas, and was able to improvise across disparate fields with fluidity. Improvisation is a skill used in all the arts — and one could say in all good teaching, certainly in inquiry-based education. This workshop offers multiple entry points to improvisation skills, from movement and music improv to storytelling and spoken-word forms. Participants explore improvisation exercises correlated with Multiple Intelligences theory, materials that lend themselves to inter-arts exploration, and the 8th Studio Habit of Mind: Stretch and Explore. The presenter draws from her training in theater, Dalcroze Eurhythmics, Orff Schulwerk, jazz, site-specific playwriting, and spoken word, and has challenged and nurtured adult improvisers in universities, school districts and a women's prison. Come explore the passion and structure of improvisation!

### Jump Start Your Play Habits & Excavate Your Play History
*For individuals, couples, parents and teachers.*
A hands-on, enlivening workshop designed to help you access your own play resources — for use as a teacher or parent in fostering intentional play, or in other areas of your life. You'll be guided through a multiple-arts experience and in mapping your play history. Teachers may find it relevant to preparing play environments, observing and extending students' play, and co-playing; parents and couples may be inspired to extend their play into new areas, recognize play offers, and excavate old interests. Consider this: research consistently shows that children and teens with ample unassisted play are among the brightest, happiest, most cooperative and resilient. Adults who stay in touch with their own playfulness help bring out their whole family's play potential. *(Available in a teacher-focused or a parent/couple-focused version.)*

### Setting Up Play Areas — Adult Playscapes
Can you say play deficit? What would it be like to come home to a variety of play spaces in your home and neighborhood — go to the one that draws you in the moment, and play, immediately? If that were possible, would your impulse to play (with art materials, words, music, dance, games) arise more often? This is a chance to restore your level of play via a fun, three-step process: recharge old interests with tools to integrate play and work, and map out ways to give your home — no matter what size — the playscapes you need.

### Setting Up Play Areas at Home for Children
Parents are increasingly asked to support their children's interests — helping them find their passions, providing structured and unstructured play, independent exploratory learning. Yet there are often blank looks when parents first hear this description of home learning. Where do parents find the tools and skills to build on their children's interests? This workshop offers a hands-on experience of setting up play areas at home. Considerations of space, materials, and how to be a "guide on the side" will help you support passion time, intentional playtime and maker spaces in your home — and your students' homes.

### Guided Play in Practice
Guided play is a successful pedagogical approach that helps children narrow down what they need to pay attention to, so they can focus on the elements relevant to a particular learning goal — while still being actively involved and directing much of their own learning. Research suggests that playful approaches incorporating adult scaffolding are more likely to lead to academic outcomes with very young learners than either direct instruction or free play. The presenter has led kindergartners in design-thinking invention projects, third graders in original music composition about solar science, middle schoolers in shadow-puppetry scenes about their own lives, and adults in slam poetry. She holds an MA in interdisciplinary arts education and is an advocate of the full continuum of play-based learning.

### Playful Schools: From Constructivism to Activity Centers to Free Play
How playful is your school? Geared for those who teach and administrate programs for toddlers through third-graders, this workshop focuses on practices that promote thoughtful, constructivist play opportunities, the continuum of play-based learning pedagogies, and the roots of our own concepts of play. The presenter has taught newborns to PhD students and is passionate about creating workspace for a mix of participants from across the education field. Through hands-on work and group discussion, we inform one another in rich, interdisciplinary ways.

### Applying a Play Rubric to Your Planning and Assessment
We recognize a writer's playful use of language — but do we know how to nurture it in our students? We recognize the joy and humor in physically playful people — but do we understand its importance to social and emotional health, and can we develop it? To be an effective guide bridging students' play passions with new experiences, we need to identify developmental, cultural and artistic factors within a specific play experience. Within the broad categories of physical, social/emotional and intellectual play there are culturally and artistically specific kinds of play, which can be broken down further by developmental level. Understanding play signals and using a play rubric helps teachers focus on specific aspects of play, then choose exercises, frameworks and thinking routines that address both play habits and arts/academic content.
